Week of 5/9/2020 by econScope

  1. Home
  2. Weekly Updates

Posted on May 10, 2020

3.2 million more people filed for unemployment benefits last week, reaching a total of 33 million.

The month of April experienced a 14.7% unemployment rate, with 51.3% of the population unemployed, and 78.3% of those unemployed are temporary layoffs. The health care sector lost 1.4 million jobs. Historically, April generated the most tax revenue; however, this year the federal government has an April budget deficit of $737 billion, which is more than twice the amount of any other month in the past 40 years. California is suffering from a $54.3 billion detriment to the state budget, with future predictions of an 18% unemployment rate and a 25.5% decline in personal income tax revenue in the next fiscal year.

J. Crew filed for bankruptcy on May 4, becoming the first major retailer to fall victim to the economic consequences of the coronavirus pandemic. Disney’s movie studios are receiving 40% of the revenue earned last year. Factories from all around the globe are experiencing declines. The U.S. nonfarm sector lost more than 20 million jobs in April, with service businesses experiencing the most drastic fall in activity. Furthermore, house prices are increasing as both demand and supply in the house market decrease. In China, exports increased by 3.5% while imports decreased by 14.2%.

As of now, the value of lives saved by social distancing as estimated by epidemiological models exceeds the economic cost. However, we don’t know how long until the economic cost will surpass the need for safety measures. On May 6, the U.S. Treasury decided to increase auction sizes of longer-dated securities and will issue a new 20-year bond at an initial offering of $20 billion. The EU economy will shrink by more than 7%, surpassing its 2009 recession of 4.5%. Greece, Spain, and Italy will suffer the most damage, as they are projected to contract close to 10%. The eurozone economy in the year 2024 will be 3% smaller than the original prediction. Government debt as a percentage of GDP is seeing levels higher than the previous decade.
