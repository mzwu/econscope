Posted on October 4, 2020

Upon receiving numerous complaints from small business owners, the Treasury Department announced that it would begin forgiving loans under the Paycheck Protection Program.

Forgiveness requests should be expected to be processed sometime this week or early next week, a short turnaround time for loans below $2 million. Since early August, nearly 100,000 business owners have seeked loan forgiveness. These requests constitute 2% of all loans, totaling to $525 billion in worth. Some officials are encouraging small business owners to apply for loan forgiveness, but others fear that the forgiveness process is too complex and slow. Overall, recent surveys show that many businesses across the nation could likely close down without immediate financial support.

The Labor Department reported that 661,000 jobs were added in September, counting toward the 11.4 million jobs that were replaced of the 22 million jobs that were lost in March and April of 2020. The official unemployment rate for the month of September was reported to be 7.9%, which was a 0.5% drop from the previous month. Even so, layoffs continue to persist, worrying economists with a sign of prolonged unemployment for many across the country. In the third quarter, the amount of long-term unemployed workers was 23% Latino and 21% Black, both percentages of which are disproportionately high compared to their population makeup. Restrictions are easing at a slower pace than during early summer, so many unemployed workers are having a tough time seeking work opportunities.

House Speaker Nancy Pelosi and Treasury Secretary Steven Mnuchin discussed Tuesday morning to try to reach an economic relief bill deal before the election takes place. Democrats released a new $2.2 trillion package proposal, marking a decrease from their prior proposals. After their conversation, the two officials expect to reach a deal sometime this week. Democrats are urging Speaker Pelosi to settle on a deal quickly, while conservatives are worried that spending too much before the election will result in low conservative voter turnout. The $2.2 trillion proposal includes another round of direct individual stimulus checks, a renewal of the $600 weekly federal unemployment benefits, and an extension of the Paycheck Protection Program. Other uses include state and local government support, increased food stamp benefits, and funds for businesses greatly affected by the COVID-19 pandemic.

Although U.S. consumer spending rose in August, the Commerce Department reported a 2.7% decrease in U.S. personal income in August. This drop was likely due to expiration of federal weekly unemployment benefits and slow job growth. As economists analyze trends in spending and saving over the past eight months, they can see a significant dip in spending and spike in saving in April. After that point, spending and saving levels quickly shifted toward the pre-pandemic direction. Since then, indicators of economic recovery have displayed gradually slowing growth. It is unlikely that the U.S. will undergo a second recession, but at this point economists tend to concur that the remaining recovery will take place over the long term.

Chinese President Xi Jinping has advocated for domestic circulation, which consists of supporting local supply chains and domestic consumption. By use of this policy, the Chinese economy will be more resilient to trade disruptions such as the U.S.-China trade war or the COVID-19 pandemic. Domestic circulation will also allow China to be more self-sufficient. However, many of the goods made in China have little value to Chinese citizens.
